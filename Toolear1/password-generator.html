NIST Guidelines: What Makes a Strong Password?

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) sets the standards for digital security. Their latest guidelines have shifted the focus from "complexity" to "length".

1. Length is King

A short password with symbols (e.g., P@ss!) is much weaker than a long password with just letters (e.g., correcthorsebatterystaple). Every additional character exponentially increases the number of possible combinations. We recommend a length of at least 16 characters for critical accounts.

2. Randomness (Entropy)

Humans are bad at being random. We tend to pick patterns on the keyboard (qwerty) or common words. A truly random string generated by a computer has high "entropy," meaning it contains no patterns for a hacker to exploit.

3. Avoid Substitution Tricks

Replacing 'a' with '@' or 'o' with '0' is not clever. Hackers' dictionaries ('Rainbow Tables') already account for these common substitutions. It is better to have a random string than a "clever" word.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it safe to generate passwords online?
Generally, you should be cautious. However, Toolear is safe because we use Client-Side Generation. The code runs in your browser, not on our server. To verify this, you can load the page, turn off your internet connection, and still generate passwords. This proves no data is being sent out.
What is a good password length?
For most online accounts, 12-16 characters is the recommended balance between security and usability. For extremely sensitive data (like crypto wallets), go for 20+ characters. Anything below 8 characters is considered vulnerable.
Why shouldn't I use words?
Dictionary attacks use lists of common words to crack passwords. Even combining words (e.g., "BlueSkyHouse") is easier to crack than a random string (e.g., "9x#L2v!m") because the "search space" for words is much smaller than for random characters.
How do I remember these passwords?
You shouldn't try to memorize random strong passwords. We highly recommend using a Password Manager (like Bitwarden, 1Password, or LastPass). You only need to remember one "Master Password," and the software handles the rest.
